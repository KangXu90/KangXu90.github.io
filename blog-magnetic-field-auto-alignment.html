Painless Magnetic Field Alignment: An Automated Bias-Field System for NV-center Experiments

Published May 29, 2026

Aligning a permanent-magnet bias field to the nitrogen-vacancy axis is one of the most tedious daily chores in diamond quantum sensing. This note describes a motorized, software-controlled module that sweeps the magnet position in X, Y, and Z, reads the photoluminescence response, and finds the optimal field automatically — turning a frustrating manual ritual into a repeatable scan.

Motivation

Optically addressable spin defects — nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers in diamond, and, increasingly, spin defects in van der Waals materials[1] — all rely on a well-controlled static magnetic bias field. The approach here was inspired by that broader line of work, and the same alignment problem shows up across these platforms.

Many NV-center experiments need a static bias field of a few hundred gauss aligned along the NV symmetry axis. A small permanent magnet on a translation/rotation stage is the usual, affordable solution, but getting it aligned is painful: the photoluminescence (PL) and ODMR contrast depend sensitively on both the magnitude and the direction of the field, and the optimum sits in a narrow region of a multi-dimensional space. Tuning several micrometer knobs by hand, watching a counter, and iterating is slow, hard to reproduce, and easy to drift out of overnight.

The goal of this project was simple: make magnetic field alignment painless. Instead of turning knobs, the magnet sits on motorized stages, and a small piece of software sweeps its position while recording the PL signal, so the best alignment is found — and can be returned to — on demand.

The Mechanical Module

The magnet is mounted on a stack of motorized translation stages that move it in X, Y, and Z relative to the diamond, with an angular mount that sets the field direction. The design was first laid out as a 3D model and then built into the existing confocal setup inside the laser enclosure.

A key feature of the mount is that the field orientation is reconfigurable, so the same module adapts to different host materials and defect geometries without rebuilding the setup. For a (100)-cut diamond, the NV symmetry axes sit at the magic angle of about 54.7° from the surface normal — the angle between the <111> and <100> crystal directions — so the magnet is set to roughly 54.7° in elevation to align the bias field along an NV axis. The mount can instead be reconfigured to point the field straight down, normal to the sample, which suits (111)-oriented diamond, where an NV axis lies along the surface normal, as well as out-of-plane defects in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN).

Software and GUI

Motion control and data acquisition are wrapped in a MATLAB App. The GUI connects to the motor controllers, exposes per-axis scan ranges (min, max, number of points, speed) for X, Y, and Z, and shows the live PL map as the scan runs. A status panel reports the current target position, and a Set to cursor button lets the user jump the magnet straight to a chosen point on the map.

The alignment app was built on top of the lab's existing instrument-control code, reusing the motor drivers, signal-generator configuration, galvo control, and counter-acquisition routines already used for confocal imaging and pulse sequences. Keeping the new magnetalignment app inside the same codebase means alignment scans share the same hardware abstractions as the rest of the experiment.

XYZ Auto Sweeping

With the module and GUI in place, alignment becomes a search rather than a chore. A typical workflow is:

  • Do a coarse Z sweep to set the field magnitude (Z largely controls magnet-to-sample distance, and hence field strength).
  • Run an XY raster at the chosen Z to map how PL varies with field direction in the plane.
  • Read the optimum off the live map, click Set to cursor, and refine with a finer local sweep.

Because every move is logged and repeatable, the same alignment can be recovered exactly the next day instead of being re-discovered by hand.

Results

The scans below show the system working across the field range used in our experiments. A Z sweep around 500 gauss produces a clear, well-defined PL minimum that pinpoints the best magnet-to-sample distance, while an XY raster near 400 gauss reveals a bright, well-defined alignment region rather than a vague plateau.

Optical (PL) alignment is then confirmed spectroscopically. ODMR scans at the aligned position show deep, well-resolved resonance dips — the signature of a bias field that is both the right magnitude and cleanly aligned to the NV axis.

Practical Lessons

  • Separate the search: use Z mainly for field magnitude, then sweep XY for direction.
  • Start coarse and zoom in; a fine raster over the whole range wastes time far from the optimum.
  • Show the PL map live — seeing the gradient makes it obvious which way to refine.
  • Log every move so a good alignment can be reproduced exactly, not rediscovered by hand.
  • Always confirm an optical optimum with ODMR; maximum PL and clean resonance should agree.

Conclusion

A motorized magnet stage plus a small control app turns one of the most tedious parts of NV-center work into a quick, repeatable scan. The same idea — sweep a physical parameter, read the signal, and let the software find the optimum — generalizes well beyond field alignment, and it pays for itself the first time you need to recover yesterday's setting without touching a knob.
